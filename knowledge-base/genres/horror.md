# Horror Genre Conventions

This knowledge base provides guidance for writing Horror novels. 
Horror aims to elicit a psychological or physical reaction of fear, dread, or disgust from the reader.

## Core Elements
1. **The Monster / The Unknown:** The source of the fear. It can be physical (a vampire), psychological (madness), or environmental (a haunted house).
2. **Isolation:** The protagonist must be cut off from help, either physically (trapped in a cabin) or psychologically (no one believes them).
3. **Atmosphere and Dread:** Horror relies heavily on setting a mood. The buildup of dread is often more terrifying than the actual monster.
4. **Vulnerability:** The characters must be genuinely at risk. If the protagonist is an unkillable superhero, it isn't horror.

## The Mechanics of Fear
- **Terror vs. Horror:** *Terror* is the feeling of dread and anticipation before the scary event happens. *Horror* is the feeling of revulsion or shock *after* or *during* the scary event. Effective horror balances both.
- **The Uncanny:** Taking something familiar and making it slightly, unsettlingly wrong (e.g., a doll that moves when you aren't looking).
- **The Reveal vs. The Unseen:** What the reader imagines is often scarier than what you describe. Delay showing the monster fully for as long as possible.

## Common Subgenres
- **Psychological Horror:** The threat is internal, focusing on paranoia, madness, and the unreliability of perception.
- **Supernatural/Paranormal:** Involves ghosts, demons, vampires, or entities beyond scientific understanding.
- **Cosmic Horror (Lovecraftian):** Focuses on the fear of the incomprehensible and the insignificance of humanity in the vast universe.
- **Body Horror:** The horror derives from unnatural changes, mutations, or destruction of the human body.
- **Folk Horror:** Rooted in ancient traditions, isolation in nature, and rural cults.

## Reader Expectations
- **Atmosphere:** Readers want to feel the creeping dread.
- **Consequences:** In horror, not everyone survives. The stakes must be lethal or worse than lethal (e.g., loss of soul or sanity).
- **The Rules of the Monster:** Even supernatural entities must have rules or limitations, or else the protagonist has no chance to fight back or escape.

## Common Pitfalls (What to Avoid)
- **Over-Explaining:** Explaining the exact biological mechanics of the ancient demon ruins the mystery. Fear thrives in the unknown.
- **Jump Scares in Text:** "Suddenly a cat jumped out!" does not work in literature. You must build dread through pacing and atmosphere.
- **Invincible Monsters:** If the monster has absolutely no weaknesses and the protagonist has zero agency, the story becomes a depressing slog rather than a terrifying struggle.
