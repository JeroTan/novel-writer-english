# Science Fiction Genre Conventions

This knowledge base provides guidance for writing Science Fiction novels. 
Science fiction explores the "what if?" of technological, scientific, or societal changes.

## Core Elements
1. **The Novum (The "New Thing"):** The central scientific or technological difference from our world (e.g., FTL travel, genetic engineering, AI sentience).
2. **Worldbuilding:** Must be logically consistent. If you introduce a technology, you must explore its societal, economic, and personal implications.
3. **Themes:** Often explores human nature through the lens of the "other" or the "future." Common themes include humanity's place in the universe, the ethics of creation, and societal evolution.

## Hard vs. Soft Sci-Fi
- **Hard Sci-Fi:** Prioritizes scientific accuracy and logic. The technology is rooted in known physics (or plausible extrapolations). The "how" is as important as the "why."
- **Soft Sci-Fi:** Prioritizes character, society, and philosophy. The technology is a vehicle for the plot, not the focus. (e.g., *Star Wars*, *Dune*).

## Common Subgenres
- **Space Opera:** Grand scale, interstellar conflict, melodrama, romance, and adventure (e.g., *The Expanse*).
- **Cyberpunk:** High tech, low life. Focuses on hackers, megacorporations, cybernetics, and dystopian futures (e.g., *Neuromancer*).
- **Post-Apocalyptic:** Life after a cataclysmic event. Focuses on survival, resource scarcity, and rebuilding society.
- **First Contact:** The initial meeting between humanity and extraterrestrial life.

## Reader Expectations
- **Internal Consistency:** If a ship takes 3 weeks to cross a system in chapter 1, it shouldn't take 3 hours in chapter 20 without explanation.
- **Sense of Wonder:** Readers want to be transported to fascinating, imaginative new worlds.
- **Plausibility:** Even in soft sci-fi, the rules of the universe must make sense within the context of the story.

## Common Pitfalls (What to Avoid)
- **Info-Dumping:** Do not halt the plot to explain how the hyperdrive works. Integrate explanations naturally into the action ("As you know, Bob...").
- **Techno-Babble:** Using scientific-sounding words without meaning. If the tech doesn't make sense, readers will lose immersion.
- **The "Magic" Fix:** Using technology as a flawless "Deus Ex Machina" to solve plot problems without setup or cost. Technology should create as many problems as it solves.
