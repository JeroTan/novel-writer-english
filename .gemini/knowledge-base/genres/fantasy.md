# Fantasy Genre Conventions

This knowledge base provides guidance for writing Fantasy novels.
Fantasy relies on magic, supernatural elements, and entirely fabricated worlds that operate on their own rules.

## Core Elements
1. **Magic Systems:** Can be "Hard" (strict rules, costs, predictable) or "Soft" (mysterious, wondrous, unpredictable). Both require consistency.
2. **Worldbuilding:** The setting is often a character itself. Details like geography, history, politics, religion, and ecology must be developed.
3. **The Quest/Journey:** A common trope where the protagonist must travel to achieve a goal, growing along the way.

## Common Subgenres
- **High/Epic Fantasy:** Set in entirely fictional worlds (e.g., *Lord of the Rings*). Large stakes, often world-ending.
- **Low/Urban/Contemporary Fantasy:** Set in the real world but with magical elements hidden or integrated (e.g., *Harry Potter*, *Dresden Files*).
- **Dark Fantasy/Grimdark:** Gritty, morally ambiguous, often violent worlds where heroes are flawed and endings aren't always happy.

## Reader Expectations
- **Internal Logic:** Even if magic exists, it must have limitations. Superman without Kryptonite is boring. Magic without a cost removes tension.
- **Immersion:** Readers want to feel like they inhabit this world. Use all five senses to describe unique flora, fauna, and architecture.
- **Growth:** The protagonist must start weak or ignorant and grow in power and understanding.

## Common Pitfalls
- **Deus Ex Machina:** Using magic to magically solve an unsolvable problem at the last minute without prior setup.
- **Info-Dumping:** Starting the book with a 10-page history of the world's creation myth. Show the world through the character's interactions with it.
